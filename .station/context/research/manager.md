# Manager Research

## Product Story

“I can scan the team in 10 seconds.”

“I can spot the 2 reps worth coaching today.”

“I can see whether the issue is activity, conversion, or deal value.”

“I can open the drill-down only when I need proof.”

## Demo

Use the dashboard to answer a manager’s unspoken question: “Show me the reps I should care about right now.” Then immediately offer the next question: “Want me to prove why they’re behind?” That mirrors how managers actually use dashboards to prioritize coaching time and move from raw activity to decisions.

### Scenarios

Rep A is at 92% of goal with low activity but high close rate. The manager sees that the problem is pipeline creation, not selling skill.

Rep B is at 70% of goal with strong activity but weak close rate. The manager sees a coaching issue in qualification or execution.

Rep C is at 85% of goal but projected to miss because average deal size is shrinking. The manager sees a deal-quality or pricing issue.

Rep D is on pace overall, but activity has dropped sharply week over week. The manager sees an early warning before the month is lost.

Rep E has high volume, low results, and stale updates. The manager sees both performance and hygiene risk.

These examples work because they connect the same dashboard to different managerial decisions: coach volume, coach conversion, coach deal size, or simply watch the trend.

### What to avoid

Also avoid trying to prove every capability at once. In a demo, one clear diagnosis path is more persuasive than ten weak ones, because it shows the product can support a decision rather than just display data.

### Script

Suggested script
A clean demo flow would be:

Open with the rep list and call out the few reps that need attention.

Ask, “Do you want me to show why these two are behind?”

Drill into one rep and show the diagnostic summary.

Tie the summary to a coaching action.

Repeat once with a different pattern so it feels broadly useful.

That gives you a simple proof loop: identify, explain, act.

Best proof points
The strongest proof points for this audience are not sophistication or completeness. They are speed, clarity, and confidence in coaching decisions.

## Features

### Rep List

Pace to date versus expected pace for the current month.

A simple risk state, such as on track, watch, or behind.

One leading driver summary, not a long stack of KPIs, such as activity pace or conversion rate.

Last meaningful activity or last updated time, if stale data matters operationally.

At the top level, the manager wants a list that helps them triage. The best view is not “all the metrics,” but “who is healthy, who is drifting, and who is failing for a specific reason”

### Drill Down

Core Question: "Why is this person behind?"

The best diagnostic set is small and causal: activity pace, asks, closing ratio, average account value

That structure supports the manager’s real job, which is identifying whether the gap is caused by low volume, poor conversion, smaller deal size, or weak follow-through

Also avoid showing only activity counts without outcome context. Managers care less about “how busy” a rep was than whether the activity is producing meetings, opportunities, and sold revenue.

Best filter logic
Use the dashboard as a prioritization tool, not a complete record. A rep should bubble up if they are behind pace, projected to miss goal, or have a sharp mismatch between activity and results, such as high activity but weak conversion.

A good rule is to highlight only the few dimensions that explain most performance variance: volume, conversion, and deal size. Everything else should be one click away, not on the main list.
